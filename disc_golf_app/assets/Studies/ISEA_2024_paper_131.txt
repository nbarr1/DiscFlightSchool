Introduction 
Recently, the game of disc golf has seen rapid growth, with a 55%  increase in Professional 
Disc Golf Association (PDGA) memberships in 2021 [1]. Recent papers have predicted the 
dynamics of golf discs, such as the model from Giljarhus et al. [2], but studies focusing on 
player performance have yet to be conducted. In 2017, Sasakawa et al. [3] compared ultimate 
forehand throws, a biomechanically similar action to forehand disc golf throws,  from skilled 
and unskilled players. This abstract considers the performance characteristics of both 
backhand and forehand disc golf throws and relates them to player skill level. 
 
Methods 
Twenty-eight participants with varying skill levels took part in this study. Twenty-three of the 
participants were male and 5 were female . The official PDGA rating system  was used to 
quantify the participants’ skill levels [4]. The average male and female PDGA ratings from this 
study were 936 and 855, respectively, with higher ratings correlating to better-scoring players. 
The average participant for each sex was considered an above average amateur player [4]. 
The participants gave informed consent to take part in this study, and the study was 
conducted in accordance with the ethical standards of the University of Waterloo. 
The players threw an Innova D isc Golf Destroyer (Innova D isc Golf, Rock Hill, USA) 
driver, with a mass of 173.5 grams, into a net. The players were allowed to take their preferred 
run-up to the throw. Five backhand and 5 forehand throws were recorded for each participant. 
To quantify performance, a single high -speed Photron SA1 (Photron, Tokyo, Japan) 
camera, with a frame rate of 6250 frames/second and resolution of 1024 x 896 pixels, was 
mounted above the participants to record the disc motion at release. A minimum of 30 frames 
were used to estimate the speed and spin  of the disc . Due to the single camera setup, the 
players were asked to throw the discs horizontally. 
To find the disc’s initial speed, it was assumed that the velocity of the disc was entirely 
within the horizontal plane and that the disc did not have any initial wobble. A circle was fitted 
around the disc in each  frame to locate the disc centre and to estimate the pixel-to-metre 
conversion for a given throw . Regression lines relating the disc position (one for each of the 
frame axes) to time were calculated. The slopes of these lines were taken as the longitudinal 
and lateral velocity components to calculate the disc speed. 
Under the same assumptions as above, the disc spin was estimated by tracking the 
rotation of circular markers located on the topside of the disc. The angles of lines from the 
markers to the disc centre were determined in each frame. Regression lines  relating these 
angles to time were calculated and the average slope was taken as the disc spin.

Results and Discussion 
Table 1 shows the average launch speed and spin of t he disc for varying skill levels.  
Within skill -level groupings, backhand throws produce significantly more spin but similar 
speed to forehand throws. In general, the higher -skilled players produced more speed and 
spin than the lower-skilled players. This agrees with the findings of Sasakawa et al. [3], where 
more skilful players generated higher speed and spin in forehand ultimate throws.

Table 2 shows  the correlation coefficients of speed and spin with PDGA rating  using 
the average backhand and forehand  throws of each participant . Backhand speed and spin 
showed stronger correlations to PDGA rating than their forehand counterparts. Forehand spin 
for male participants was the weakest correlation and not significant. Most participants noted 
backhand as their preferred throwing style, meaning for some players, if the forehand is used 
sparingly during play, forehand throwing performance may not correlate well to their rating.

To improve upon this work, more female participants should be included to produce 
statistically meaningful results for both sexes. Additionally, a stereo camera system can be 
used to collect the complete three-dimensional launch conditions of the disc . A trajectory 
model [2] can then be used to give further insight into the performance of the throw.
